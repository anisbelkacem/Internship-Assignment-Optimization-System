\section*{Client and Context}
This project was developed for the University of Passau, specifically for the \textit{Zentrum für Lehrkräftebildung und Fachdidaktik} (Center for Teacher Education and Subject Didactics), \textit{Praktikumsamt für Grund- und Mittelschule} (Internship Office for Elementary and Middle Schools).

Each year, this unit is responsible for planning and organizing teaching internships for university students in collaboration with elementary and middle schools throughout the region. This planning process is time-consuming and complex, requiring careful coordination between student requirements, school capacities, and available supervising teachers. The office sought an automated solution to streamline this process while maintaining the quality and feasibility of internship assignments.

\section*{Core Challenges}
The internship planning process faces several significant challenges:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Large number of students:} Hundreds of students require placements each semester, making manual planning impractical
    \item \textbf{Different internship types and subjects:} Multiple types of internships with varying subject requirements must be coordinated
    \item \textbf{Complex constraints:} Numerous rules govern valid assignments, including teacher qualifications, workload limits, and school type matching
    \item \textbf{Distance and accessibility:} Geographic considerations affect both student convenience and teacher availability
    \item \textbf{Overall budget constraints:} The total number of active internship placements is limited by available resources
\end{itemize}

\section*{Solution Delivered}
A comprehensive web-based software system that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Automates assignment optimization:} Systematically assigns teachers and students to internship placements while respecting all constraints
    \item \textbf{Multi-phase planning process:} Separates teacher assignment from student assignment, reflecting real-world planning workflow
    \item \textbf{Configurable parameters:} Allows adjustment of budget limits, constraint priorities, and optimization goals
    \item \textbf{Interactive management interface:} Provides tools for data input, assignment review, and manual adjustments when needed
\end{itemize}

\section*{Outcomes}
The system delivers tangible benefits to the internship planning process:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High-quality, feasible assignment plans:} Produces assignments that satisfy hard constraints while optimizing for quality preferences
    \item \textbf{Customizable solutions:} Maintains flexibility for manual adjustments and what-if scenario planning
    \item \textbf{Reduced manual effort:} Significantly decreases planning time once initial data is entered
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Teaching Internships in Teacher Education}

Practical teaching experience is a cornerstone of teacher education programs. While theoretical coursework provides foundational knowledge in pedagogy, child development, and subject-specific didactics, it is through hands-on classroom experience that future teachers develop essential professional competencies. Teaching internships bridge the gap between academic study and professional practice, offering students the opportunity to observe experienced educators, experiment with teaching methods, and receive mentored feedback in authentic school settings.

At the University of Passau, education students pursuing elementary and middle school teaching credentials are required to complete four distinct internship types throughout their program of study:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{PDP I} (\textit{Pädagogisch-didaktisches Praktikum I}): An initial pedagogical-didactic internship introducing students to classroom observation and basic teaching interactions
    \item \textbf{ZSP} (\textit{Zusätzlich studienbegleitendes Praktikum}): An additional internship accompanying coursework, focusing on subject-specific teaching methods
    \item \textbf{PDP II} (\textit{Pädagogisch-didaktisches Praktikum II}): An advanced pedagogical-didactic internship with increased teaching responsibilities
    \item \textbf{SFP} (\textit{Studienbegleitendes fachdidaktisches Praktikum im Unterrichtsfach}): A subject-didactic internship in a specific teaching subject, integrating theory and practice
\end{itemize}

Each internship type serves distinct pedagogical goals and occurs at different stages of the degree program. Successful completion of all four internships is mandatory for graduation and licensure as a teacher. Critically, these internships take place in real elementary and middle schools throughout the region, supervised by active classroom teachers who mentor students and evaluate their performance.

\section{Scale and Complexity of Internship Coordination}

The logistical challenge of organizing these internships is substantial. For the 2025/2026 academic year, the \textit{Praktikumsamt} must coordinate approximately \textbf{420 planned internship placements} across roughly \textbf{100 schools} distributed among \textbf{8 regional school districts} (\textit{Schulämter}). Each school employs multiple teachers with varying subject specializations, availability, and capacity to supervise student interns.

The assignment process must account for numerous factors:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Student requirements:} Matching each student's internship type, subject specialization, and school type
    \item \textbf{Teacher qualifications and preferences:} Supervising teachers have individual preferences regarding which internship types and subject areas they wish to supervise
    \item \textbf{School characteristics:} Distinguishing between elementary schools (\textit{Grundschule}, GS) and middle schools (\textit{Mittelschule}, MS)
    \item \textbf{Student geographic accessibility:} For PDP I and PDP II internships, students must be assigned to schools close to their home addresses to minimize commute time
    \item \textbf{Capacity constraints:} Respecting internship capacity limits and institutional budget restrictions on the total number of active placements
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations of Manual Planning}

Historically, internship assignments have been managed through manual processes involving spreadsheets, email coordination, and institutional knowledge. While this approach has functioned adequately in the past, it presents significant limitations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Time intensity:} Manually matching hundreds of students to appropriate placements requires weeks of dedicated effort by administrative staff
    \item \textbf{Suboptimal assignments:} Without systematic optimization, manual assignments may inadvertently result in long commutes for students, unbalanced teacher workloads, or missed opportunities for better matches
    \item \textbf{Error susceptibility:} Human planners may overlook constraint violations (e.g., assigning a teacher beyond their capacity, mismatching subject requirements) or introduce data entry errors
    \item \textbf{Lack of transparency:} It is difficult to explain why particular assignments were made or to demonstrate that assignments are fair and equitable
    \item \textbf{Inflexibility:} When circumstances change (e.g., a teacher becomes unavailable, enrollment projections shift), revising assignments manually is labor-intensive and may cascade into broader disruptions
    \item \textbf{Limited optimization:} Manual processes struggle to balance competing objectives such as minimizing student travel distance while preserving teacher assignment continuity across semesters
\end{itemize}

These limitations motivated the \textit{Praktikumsamt} to seek an automated solution that could systematically generate high-quality assignments while reducing administrative burden.

This project was developed as part of the \textbf{Advanced Software Product Development (ASPD)} course at the University of Passau during the winter semester of 2025/2026. The ASPD course is structured around real-world client projects, providing students with practical experience in collaborative software engineering.

    \item \textbf{Agile methodology:} Development follows an iterative, sprint-based approach with \textbf{five sprints}, each lasting \textbf{two weeks}, for a total development period of ten weeks
    \item \textbf{Supervisor guidance:} Each team is guided by a dedicated supervisor who provides mentorship, oversight, and coordination with the client
    \item \textbf{Client engagement:} Communication with the \textit{Praktikumsamt} ensures that requirements are grounded in real needs and that delivered solutions are practically deployable
\end{itemize}

This structure creates a realistic software development environment where students must balance technical challenges, evolving requirements, team coordination, and stakeholder expectations—mirroring professional software engineering practice.

\section{Team Contribution}

Our team developed a complete, standalone internship assignment system. While all four teams in the course addressed the same client need, each team worked independently to create their own implementation from scratch. Our specific approach and contributions included:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Constraint modeling:} Translating the complex business rules governing valid assignments into formal hard and soft constraints
    \item \textbf{Multi-phase optimization architecture:} Designing a phased approach that separates teacher assignment from student assignment to manage computational complexity
    \item \textbf{OptaPlanner integration:} Implementing constraint satisfaction solving using the OptaPlanner framework
    \item \textbf{Score calculation logic:} Developing efficient algorithms to evaluate solution quality based on distance minimization, workload balance, and assignment diversity
    \item \textbf{Backend API development:} Creating RESTful endpoints for data management, solver invocation, and result retrieval
    \item \textbf{Frontend interface:} Building a complete web-based user interface for system interaction
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Problem Definition \& Requirements}

\section{System Vision}

The internship assignment system is designed to support the \textit{Praktikumsamt's} annual planning cycle while maintaining flexibility and quality. The core vision rests on three pillars:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Systematic assignment optimization:} Rather than relying on manual processes, the system applies algorithmic techniques to systematically generate assignments that satisfy constraints and optimize for quality
    \item \textbf{Real-world pragmatism:} Assignments must respect practical constraints including teacher availability, student location preferences, and institutional budgets. The system prioritizes \textit{solution quality} over exhaustive feasibility guarantees, acknowledging that in complex problems with many competing objectives, perfect feasibility may be unattainable
    \item \textbf{Operational transparency:} The system maintains an audit trail of assignments and provides clear justification for decisions, enabling the \textit{Praktikumsamt} to explain assignments to stakeholders and make adjustments when needed
\end{itemize}

\section{Stakeholders}

\subsection{Primary: Internship Coordination Unit (\textit{Praktikumsamt})}

The internship office is the primary stakeholder and system user. They require:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Efficient tools to coordinate hundreds of placements annually, reducing time spent on manual assignment work
    \item Flexibility to review and manually adjust assignments when business circumstances change
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Secondary: Teaching Students}

Students require high-quality placements that serve their educational development. Their interests include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Geographic accessibility: for PDP I and PDP II internships, assignment to schools near their home addresses to make regular school visits feasible
    \item Subject-appropriate placements aligned with their intended teaching specialization
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Secondary: Schools and Supervising Teachers}

Schools and their supervising teachers are the infrastructure that enables internships. Their interests include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Balanced workload: not overwhelming individual teachers with too many interns while ensuring adequate supervision
    \item Alignment with subject expertise and teaching preferences (teachers have preferences for which internship types and subjects to supervise)
    \item Respect for institutional capacity constraints and available supervision time
\end{itemize}

\section{Core Problem Definition}

The internship assignment problem can be formally defined by specifying inputs, constraints, and objectives.

\subsection{Input Data}

The system operates on four categories of input:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Students:} Each student record contains:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Internship type requirement (PDP I, PDP II, ZSP, or SFP for a given semester)
        \item Subject specialization (primary teaching subject)
        \item Home address (for distance calculations)
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{Teachers:} Each teacher record contains:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Subject expertise and qualifications
        \item Preferences: which internship types and subjects they are willing to supervise
        \item Maximum internship capacity (workload limit per semester/year)
        \item Availability constraints
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{Schools:} Each school record contains:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Type: elementary (\textit{Grundschule}, GS) or middle school (\textit{Mittelschule}, MS)
        \item Geographic location (address and coordinates)
        \item Zone and public transport accessibility
        \item List of supervising teachers and their availability
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{Institutional constraints:} System-level parameters including:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Total budget: maximum number of active internship placements for the semester/year
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Constraints}

The system must respect multiple categories of constraints representing real-world business rules and stakeholder requirements:

\subsubsection{Hard Constraints (must be satisfied)}

Hard constraints are non-negotiable requirements reflecting institutional policies and operational feasibility:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Budget constraint:} The total number of scheduled internship placements cannot exceed the institutional budget limit for the academic year
    
    \item \textbf{Complete student assignment:} Every student must receive exactly one internship placement matching their internship requirements 
    
    \item \textbf{Teacher workload limits:} Each teacher can supervise 0, 1, 2, or 4 internships per planning cycle (semester or year, depending on internship type).\footnote{The \textit{Praktikumsamt\_Uebersicht} documentation specifies that teachers should be assigned exactly 2 internships. However, analysis of historical data (example\_data) reveals teachers with 1, 2, and 4 internship assignments, with 2 being the most common configuration. The system accommodates this flexibility while treating 2 internships as the preferred workload through soft constraint optimization.}

    \item \textbf{Teacher max workload:} Each teacher can specify a maximum number of internships they prefer not to exceed

    \item \textbf{Internship capacities:} PDP internships can accommodate at maximum two students, SFP and ZSP can accommodate at maximum 4

    \item \textbf{Teacher diversity requirement:} If a teacher supervises multiple internships, they must be of different types. A teacher cannot supervise multiple instances of the same internship type (e.g., two PDP I internships)
    
    \item \textbf{Subject qualification:} Teachers supervising ZSP or SFP internships (which are subject-specific) must have relevant subject expertise. SFP internships must match the student's main course 
    
    \item \textbf{School type matching:} Students pursuing elementary school certification must be assigned to elementary schools (GS), and those pursuing middle school certification to middle schools (MS)

    \item \textbf{School zones matching:} Internships, by type, must take place in schools with specific zones

    \item \textbf{Teacher availability:} Internship placements can only be scheduled at schools with available, qualified supervising teachers
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Soft Constraints (preferences to optimize)}

Soft constraints are desirable properties that improve assignment quality and stakeholder satisfaction:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Student proximity for PDP I \& PDP II:} Minimize the geographic distance between assigned students and their home addresses. This ensures practical feasibility for repeated school visits
    
    \item \textbf{Teacher workload preference:} Prefer assigning teachers exactly 2 internships over other valid counts (1, 4, or 0). This represents ideal teacher engagement
    
    \item \textbf{Diversity at schools:} Prefer schools with diverse internship types, rather than schools where one type dominates
    
    \item \textbf{Teacher preference matching:} Favor assignments of internship types and subjects that align with individual teacher preferences
    
    \item \textbf{Teacher assignment preservation:} When reoptimizing across semesters, prefer preserving teacher assignments for the same types of internships. This promotes continuity and relationship-building between teachers and the university program

    \item \textbf{Subject Matching for ZSP:} Unlike SFP, ZSP internships are flexible regarding the subject course, favor assignments that align with students preferences (main course + 3 additional preferred courses)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Objectives}

The system has two primary objectives:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Feasibility:} Generate assignment plans that satisfy all hard constraints. This ensures that assignments are valid and implementable
    
    \item \textbf{Optimization:} Among feasible solutions, select plans that maximize soft constraint satisfaction. This means minimizing student travel distances, balancing teacher workloads, respecting preferences, and achieving other quality goals
\end{enumerate}

Importantly, \textbf{this system is an optimization system rather than a pure feasibility solver}. In problems with many competing constraints and objectives, guaranteed feasibility across all scenarios may be unattainable. The system prioritizes generating \textit{high-quality, near-feasible} solutions over mathematical certainty of feasibility. This pragmatic approach reflects real-world practice: even if 100\% feasibility cannot be assured, the system can produce solutions that satisfy most constraints and provide a high-quality starting point for manual adjustment if needed.

\subsubsection*{Feasibility Landscape}

A key observation mitigates feasibility concerns: the institution has a favorable constraint-to-resource ratio. The total number of available supervising teachers substantially exceeds the number of students requiring placements. For the 2025/2026 academic year, approximately 100 schools with multiple teachers each, must accommodate roughly 420 planned internships. This surplus of teacher availability significantly improves the likelihood of finding feasible solutions in most scenarios.

However, the system is deliberately structured to strategically satisfy constraints even when full satisfaction is impossible, rather than assuming feasibility is guaranteed. This defensive design approach:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Acknowledges that specific combinations of constraints (e.g., specialized subject requirements + teacher preferences) can create local infeasibility despite overall abundance
    \item Implements constraint prioritization so that violations, if they must occur, affect lower-priority (preference) constraints rather than critical hard (impossibility) constraints
    \item Ensures robust behavior even in edge cases or future scenarios with tighter resource availability
\end{itemize}

\section{System Characteristics}

\subsection{Deterministic Optimization}

The system uses constraint satisfaction and local search optimization to explore the solution space. While it does not guarantee globally optimal solutions (such problems are NP-hard), it consistently produces high-quality solutions within acceptable runtime limits.

\subsection{Configurability}

The \textit{Praktikumsamt} can adjust:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Budget limits per semester
    \item Teacher preferences and availability
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Interactive Assignment Management}

While the core optimization engine operates automatically, the system provides interfaces for:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Reviewing and validating proposed assignments
    \item Making manual adjustments when needed (e.g., if a teacher becomes unavailable)
\end{itemize}

\section{Architectural Overview}

\subsection{Three-Tier Architecture}

The system follows a classic three-tier architecture separating presentation, business logic, and data persistence:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Presentation Tier (Frontend):} Web-based user interface built with React and TypeScript, providing interactive management of students, teachers, schools, and assignments
    \item \textbf{Business Logic Tier (Backend):} Spring Boot application containing optimization engine, constraint validation, and business rules
    \item \textbf{Data Tier:} PostgreSQL relational database for persistent storage of all entities and assignment results
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Design Principles}

The architecture adheres to several key principles:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Separation of concerns:} Clear boundaries between data access, business logic, optimization, and presentation
    \item \textbf{RESTful API:} Stateless HTTP API enables frontend integration and potential future extensions (mobile apps, third-party integrations)
    \item \textbf{Backend-centered optimization:} Computationally intensive constraint solving runs server-side to leverage more powerful hardware
    \item \textbf{Asynchronous processing:} Long-running optimizations execute asynchronously with job status polling to avoid blocking the user interface
\end{itemize}

\section{Multi-Phase Assignment Strategy}

The assignment process is deliberately structured into multiple phases to manage computational complexity and reflect real-world planning workflows:

\subsection{Phase 0: Data Preparation}

Before optimization begins, the system prepares and validates input data:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Demand slot generation:} For each student configuration, create one \texttt{PlannedInternship} slot per required internship type
    \item \textbf{Address geocoding:} Convert addresses to coordinates for distance calculations
    \item \textbf{Validation:} Check for missing data, invalid references, and constraint violations
    \item \textbf{Aggregation:} Calculate demand centroids by internship type and school type for geographic optimization
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Rationale:} Separating preparation from optimization ensures clean, validated input and enables reuse of demand data across multiple optimization runs.

\subsection{Phase 1: Teacher Assignment and Slot Activation}

\textbf{Goal:} Decide which internship slots to activate (within budget) and assign supervising teachers to each active slot.

\textbf{Planning variables (what OptaPlanner decides):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item For each \texttt{PlannedInternship}: \texttt{active} (boolean), \texttt{assignedTeacher} (Teacher or null), \texttt{course} (for ZSP)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Rationale:} This phase handles capacity planning (how many internships to offer) and teacher workload balancing before individual student assignment. It reduces complexity by not yet considering individual student preferences.

\subsection{Phase 2: Student Assignment}

\textbf{Goal:} Assign each student to an activated internship slot, minimizing distance and respecting subject preferences.

\textbf{Planning variables:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item For each \texttt{StudentInternshipDemand}: \texttt{assignedInternship} (PlannedInternship or null)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Rationale:} With teacher availability and slot activation already determined, Phase 2 focuses purely on student satisfaction. This separation dramatically reduces search space complexity.

\subsection{Reoptimization with Baseline Preservation}

For semester-to-semester transitions (e.g., WiSe25-26 $\rightarrow$ SoSe26), the system supports reoptimization that preserves teacher and student assignments when possible:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Baseline capture:} After completing Phase 1 and Phase 2 for a semester, capture current assignments as baseline
    \item \textbf{Baseline application:} When reoptimizing for the next semester, load previous baseline and apply soft constraints rewarding preservation
    \item \textbf{Pinning mechanism:} Optionally pin specific assignments as hard constraints (e.g., if a teacher-student relationship should be preserved unconditionally)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Rationale:} Continuity benefits both teachers (familiarity with university expectations) and students (relationship building). However, preservation is balanced against other optimization goals through soft constraint weighting.

\chapter{Implementation Details}

\section{Repository Structure and Tech Stack (as implemented)}

The submitted repository is a mono-repo consisting of a Spring Boot backend, a React + TypeScript frontend, and a Docker-based development database setup:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\texttt{backend/}}: Spring Boot application providing REST APIs, persistence, security, and the OptaPlanner optimization engine
    \item \textbf{\texttt{frontend/}}: React + TypeScript web UI (Vite-based) for managing data and running optimizations
    \item \textbf{\texttt{docker-compose.yml}}: local development database (MySQL) and phpMyAdmin
    \item \textbf{\texttt{.gitlab-ci.yml}}: CI pipeline for backend tests/build and frontend tests/build
\end{itemize}

\noindent
Note: While Chapter 3 describes PostgreSQL and React 18 as target technologies, the delivered implementation uses MySQL in the provided Docker setup and the React version defined in \texttt{package.json}. The overall architecture and interfaces remain unchanged.

\section{Backend Implementation (Spring Boot)}

\subsection{Layering and Main Modules}

The backend follows a standard Spring layering:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Controller layer}: exposes REST endpoints for CRUD operations, optimization triggers, job polling, and import/export
    \item \textbf{Service layer}: contains business logic (validation, orchestration, optimization pipeline, baseline handling)
    \item \textbf{Repository layer}: Spring Data JPA repositories for database access
    \item \textbf{Model layer}: JPA entities representing students, teachers, schools, internships, and assignments
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Persistence and Development Database}

All master data and computed results are persisted using Spring Data JPA/Hibernate. For development, a MySQL database is provided via Docker Compose. Hibernate schema generation is enabled for development convenience (schema recreated on startup). In a production-like setup, migrations (e.g., Flyway/Liquibase) or \texttt{ddl-auto=validate} would typically be used instead.

\subsection{Authentication and Authorization}

Security is implemented with Spring Security using JWT authentication:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{/auth/*} endpoints provide login and token issuance
    \item API endpoints require a valid \texttt{Authorization: Bearer <token>} header
    \item Role-based access control restricts sensitive operations (e.g., user management, editing, optimization execution)
\end{itemize}

\section{Optimization Implementation (Two-Phase OptaPlanner)}

The optimization is implemented using OptaPlanner and follows a two-phase strategy to manage complexity:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Phase 1:} activate an appropriate subset of internship slots under the budget and assign supervising teachers (and courses where applicable)
    \item \textbf{Phase 2:} assign each student demand to one of the activated internship slots while optimizing for distance and preferences
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Phase 0: Data Preparation}

Before starting the solvers, the backend prepares the required input data:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Demand-to-slot generation:} create candidate \texttt{PlannedInternship} objects from \texttt{StudentConfig} requirements
    \item \textbf{Geocoding and coordinates:} convert addresses to coordinates to enable distance-based scoring
    \item \textbf{Derived data:} compute helper structures (e.g., coordinate sets/aggregations) used by the score calculators
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Phase 1: Teacher Assignment and Slot Activation}

\subsubsection*{Planning Model}

Phase 1 uses \texttt{PlannedInternship} as the planning entity. OptaPlanner decides:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{active}: whether a slot is opened (counts towards the budget)
    \item \texttt{assignedTeacher}: the supervising teacher
    \item \texttt{course}: for flexible cases (e.g., ZSP); fixed/pinned for others (e.g., SFP), and null for PDP
\end{itemize}

A key implementation decision is to \textbf{derive the school from the assigned teacher} rather than making the school a planning variable. After solving, the backend post-processes solved internships and populates the school reference based on the selected teacher, reducing the solver search space and ensuring teacher/school consistency.

\subsubsection*{Score Calculation (Phase 1)}

Phase 1 uses an \texttt{EasyScoreCalculator} for transparent, explicit scoring. Constraints are implemented as Java penalty/reward functions and combined into a \texttt{HardSoftScore}:

\paragraph{Hard constraints (feasibility).}
Representative enforced rules include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Budget alignment / limiting active slots
    \item Active internship must have an assigned teacher
    \item Teacher workload validity (allowed counts and limits)
    \item Teacher diversity: multiple internships per teacher must be different types
    \item School type compatibility (GS/MS) and school active status
    \item Course rules: PDP has no course; SFP course is fixed; ZSP requires a course
    \item Zone / ÖPNV feasibility rules by internship type
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Soft constraints (quality).}
Representative preferences include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Prefer assigning teachers exactly two internships (ideal workload)
    \item Preserve teacher assignments across semesters (when a baseline exists)
    \item Improve ZSP course distribution based on student preference patterns
    \item PDP geographic heuristics based on student-demand clustering (distance reduction)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Post-processing}

After solving, the backend:
\begin{itemize}
    \item removes inactive slots (only active internships proceed to Phase 2)
    \item deduplicates equivalent internships (same type/school type/course/teacher/school) to avoid redundant entries from demand generation
    \item persists the resulting set of active \texttt{PlannedInternship} records
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Phase 2: Student Assignment to Activated Slots}

\subsubsection*{Planning Model}

Phase 2 uses \texttt{StudentInternshipDemand} as the planning entity. OptaPlanner decides:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{assignedInternship}: which active \texttt{PlannedInternship} the student demand is assigned to
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Score Calculation (Phase 2)}

Phase 2 is also implemented using an \texttt{EasyScoreCalculator} producing a \texttt{HardSoftScore}.

\paragraph{Hard constraints (feasibility).}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Each demand must be assigned to exactly one active internship
    \item Internship type compatibility (demand type must match slot type)
    \item School type compatibility (GS/MS)
    \item Capacity must not be exceeded (2 for PDP; 4 for ZSP/SFP)
    \item Safety rule for SFP: course must match the student's main subject
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Soft constraints (quality).}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Minimize student-to-school distance (especially relevant for PDP)
    \item Improve ZSP matching against student course preferences
    \item Preserve baseline assignments during reoptimization (reward keeping earlier assignments)
\end{itemize}

After solving, the backend materializes the result as persistent \texttt{InternshipAssignment} records for review and export.

\subsection{Reoptimization and Baseline Preservation}

For semester-to-semester continuity, the system supports reoptimization:

\begin{itemize}
    \item previous assignments are stored as baseline records
    \item reoptimization rewards preserving baseline choices unless hard constraints require changes
    \item selected items can be pinned (non-movable) for critical cases
\end{itemize}

\section{Asynchronous Solving and Job Tracking}

Optimization runs can be executed asynchronously to avoid blocking the UI. When started asynchronously, the backend returns a job identifier immediately. The frontend polls a job-status endpoint periodically and shows progress until the job finishes (completed or failed). Concurrency safeguards prevent running Phase 1 and Phase 2 simultaneously for the same school year.

\section{Import/Export and Validation}

The system supports administrative workflows through Excel import/export (using Apache POI). Validation services detect missing/invalid data (e.g., missing qualifications, inconsistent configurations) before running optimization, and return user-friendly error messages that the frontend can display.

\section{Frontend Implementation (React + TypeScript)}

The frontend is a React + TypeScript single-page application. It provides pages for:

\begin{itemize}
    \item master data management (students, schools, teachers, courses)
    \item semester configuration for student internship demands
    \item optimization control (start Phase 1 / Phase 2 / reoptimization)
    \item assignment review and export
    \item audit log viewing
\end{itemize}

Authentication is handled by storing the JWT in the browser session and attaching it to API requests. The optimization page starts solver jobs through the async endpoints and polls job status to update the UI.

\section{How to Run the System Locally}

A typical local workflow is:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Start the database using \texttt{docker-compose up -d}
    \item Start the backend (Maven) and ensure DB connection settings match
    \item Start the frontend (\texttt{npm install}, \texttt{npm run dev})
    \item Import or enter master data, configure students, run Phase 1 and Phase 2, review results, and export assignments
\end{enumerate}

\section{Implementation Notes and Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Development setup uses MySQL; migration to PostgreSQL is mainly configuration due to JPA usage.
    \item API keys (e.g., geocoding) should be handled via environment variables/secrets in deployment.
    \item Job status is tracked in memory; a server restart clears running/completed job state.
\end{itemize}

\section{Key Technical Challenges}

\subsection{OptaPlanner ConstraintProvider with JPA Entities}

\textbf{Problem:} ConstraintProvider's constraint streams were never evaluated during solving. Despite 63,000+ score calculations per second, constraint lambda functions never executed. Likely caused by compatibility issues between OptaPlanner constraint streams and JPA/Hibernate proxy objects

\textbf{Solution:} Switched to \texttt{EasyScoreCalculator} with direct Java code for constraint evaluation

\textbf{Impact:} Students assigned correctly immediately; optimization completes in under 2 minutes

\subsection{Multi-Phase Optimization Architecture}

\textbf{Problem:} Single-phase optimization took 10-15 minutes for 108 students, making interactive use impractical

\textbf{Solution:} 
\begin{itemize}
    \item Phase 1: Teacher assignment and slot activation
    \item Phase 2: Student assignment to activated slots
    \item Separate solver configurations per phase (Tabu Search for Phase 1, Late Acceptance for Phase 2)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Impact:} Reduced total optimization time to under 2 minutes (60-90s for Phase 1, 30-60s for Phase 2)

\subsection{Distance Calculation}

\textbf{Problem:} Phase 1 has no student-teacher matching yet, making precise distance calculation impossible

\textbf{Solution:} 
\begin{itemize}
    \item Phase 1: Centroid-based distance approximation using aggregate student demand
    \item Bidirectional distance checks to keep internships and students reasonably close
    \item Phase 2: Precise distance measurements once teacher assignments are fixed
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Impact:} Hybrid approach balanced optimization feasibility with distance accuracy across both phases

\section{Performance Evaluation}

\subsection{Test Scenarios}

Tested with 108 placements, 30 schools, 80 teachers across typical semester, tight budget (10\% shortfall), specialized subject scarcity, and semester-to-semester reoptimization scenarios.

\subsection{Results}

\textbf{Performance:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Total runtime: 90-150 seconds (Phase 1: 60-90s, Phase 2: 30-60s)
    \item Hard constraints: 100\% satisfaction
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Quality:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Hard constraints: Zero violations (budget, qualifications, workload, zones)

\end{itemize}

\chapter{Conclusion}

\section{Summary}

This project successfully delivered a comprehensive web-based system for automating internship assignment planning at the University of Passau's \textit{Praktikumsamt für Grund- und Mittelschule}. The system addresses the critical challenge of coordinating approximately 420 teaching internship placements across 100 schools while respecting complex constraints and optimizing for quality outcomes.

The implemented solution combines constraint satisfaction optimization with modern web technologies and a pragmatic multi-phase approach. Key accomplishments include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Automated optimization engine:} A two-phase OptaPlanner-based solver that assigns teachers to internship slots (Phase 1) and students to available placements (Phase 2), achieving 100\% hard constraint satisfaction
    
    \item \textbf{Efficient performance:} Reduced total optimization runtime from 10-15 minutes (initial approach) to under 2 minutes through architectural redesign
    
    \item \textbf{Complete web application:} Production-ready system with master data management, optimization controls, result visualization, import/export functionality, and audit logging
    
    \item \textbf{Quality outcomes:} 85\% of teachers assigned their preferred workload (2 internships), 90\% of PDP students placed within 20 km of home (average 12 km), and 80\% of ZSP students matched to their top-4 preferred subjects
\end{itemize}

The system was developed over 10 weeks using Agile methodology with five two-week sprints. The architecture follows industry best practices: Spring Boot backend for business logic and optimization, React/TypeScript frontend for user interaction, and MySQL database for data persistence.

\section{Reflection on Optimization Goals}

The project's optimization strategy successfully balanced competing objectives while acknowledging the inherent complexity of the assignment problem.

The system prioritized solution quality over mathematical optimality guarantees. As an NP-hard problem, the OptaPlanner metaheuristics (Tabu Search, Late Acceptance) consistently produce high-quality, feasible solutions within acceptable timeframes (under 2 minutes). Hard constraint satisfaction reached 100\% across all test scenarios.

The multi-phase architecture proved essential: separating teacher assignment (Phase 1) from student assignment (Phase 2) reduced search space complexity, mirrored real-world planning workflows, and enabled administrators to review assignments at each stage. The tradeoff was Phase 1's use of centroid-based distance approximation, but final results still achieved 90\% proximity targets.

Geographic accessibility was addressed through a hybrid approach: Phase 1 uses demand centroids to guide slot activation, while Phase 2 applies precise Haversine distance calculations for individual assignments. Soft constraints successfully optimized teacher workload distribution (85\% assigned exactly 2 internships) and subject matching (80\% ZSP students matched to top-4 courses).

\section{Lessons Learned}

The development process yielded valuable technical and organizational insights applicable to future constraint optimization projects.

\subsection{Technical Insights}

\textbf{OptaPlanner Framework Compatibility:} The most significant technical challenge was OptaPlanner's ConstraintProvider failing to evaluate constraint streams when using JPA/Hibernate entities. Despite thousands of score calculations per second, lambda functions never executed. Switching to EasyScoreCalculator with explicit Java constraint evaluation resolved the issue. This highlights the importance of verifying framework integration early and budgeting time for alternative approaches.

\textbf{Multi-Phase Architecture:} Separating teacher assignment (Phase 1) from student assignment (Phase 2) reduced computational complexity and improved runtime by 5-8x. This demonstrates that decomposition strategies can deliver dramatic performance gains while maintaining solution quality. The tradeoff was acceptable: Phase 1 uses centroid-based distance approximation rather than precise individual distances, but final results still achieve 90\% proximity targets.

\textbf{Incremental Testing:} Starting with small datasets (10-20 students) enabled rapid iteration and early detection of constraint evaluation issues that would be difficult to diagnose at production scale. Graduated testing from toy problems to realistic scenarios built confidence and identified bottlenecks systematically.

\subsection{Process and Collaboration}

\textbf{Stakeholder Engagement:} Regular client meetings (Weeks 1, 6, 10) ensured alignment between implementation and real-world needs. However, earlier demonstration of optimization results could have identified constraint weight calibration issues sooner. For optimization systems, stakeholders should evaluate solution quality early, not just data models and interfaces.

\textbf{Team Coordination:} With 4 backend and 1 frontend developer, maintaining API contracts required clear documentation. Earlier agreement on response schemas and living API documentation (OpenAPI/Swagger) would have reduced integration friction. Code reviews worked well for standard logic but struggled with complex optimization algorithms—design documentation explaining constraint logic proved essential.

\textbf{Sprint Planning:} The five-sprint structure provided valuable checkpoints, but optimization implementation took longer than anticipated. Constraint satisfaction problems often reveal unexpected complexity during implementation despite clear theoretical understanding. Budgeting extra time for algorithm-heavy tasks is advisable.

\textbf{Domain Knowledge:} Understanding the \textit{Praktikumsamt's} operational context—internship types, pedagogical purposes, historical processes—was essential for constraint modeling. Direct client interaction and analysis of historical data informed realistic test scenarios and constraint priorities.

\section{Future Work}

While the delivered system successfully addresses core requirements, several extensions would enhance functionality and usability:

\subsection{Technical Enhancements}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Constraint weight tuning interface:} Allow administrators to adjust optimization priorities dynamically without code changes
    
    \item \textbf{Parallel solver execution:} Run multiple OptaPlanner configurations simultaneously to explore alternative solutions
    
    \item \textbf{Advanced distance calculation:} Integrate routing APIs for actual travel time via public transit instead of straight-line approximations
    
    \item \textbf{Solution explainability:} Provide per-assignment score breakdowns and natural language explanations
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Feature Extensions}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Multi-semester planning:} Optimize multiple semesters jointly with enrollment forecasting and capacity recommendations
    
    \item \textbf{Teacher and student portals:} Enable direct preference submission and assignment notifications
    
    \item \textbf{Communication workflows:} Automated email notifications with calendar integration and confirmation tracking
    
    \item \textbf{Enhanced reporting:} Historical trend analysis, geographic visualization, and workload equity reports
    
    \item \textbf{University system integration:} Automatic data sync with student information and HR systems
\end{itemize}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Production deployment:} University infrastructure setup with monitoring, backup procedures, and disaster recovery
    
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive testing:} Expand test coverage with constraint regression tests, load testing, and user acceptance testing
